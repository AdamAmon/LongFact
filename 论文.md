## Abstract

Long-document summarization models frequently generate factually inconsistent content despite achieving reasonable ROUGE scores. This paper presents an end-to-end pipeline for evaluating and correcting factual consistency in summaries generated from the GovReport dataset. We propose **DCE** (Dual-Channel Evidence Retrieval with Entailment-Guided Re-Ranking), a novel retrieval strategy that combines semantic embeddings, keyword matching, lightweight re-ranking, and adaptive top-K selection to improve sentence-level factual verification. Experiments on 500 GovReport validation samples compare DCE against a standard retrieval baseline. Results show that: (1) automatic correction improves sentence-level support rate from 67.23% to 77.26% (+10.03pp) with baseline retrieval, and from 64.73% to 75.70% (+10.96pp) with DCE; (2) DCE achieves greater correction gain despite stricter initial NLI judgments, reducing false-positive ENTAILMENT by 6.16pp; (3) ROUGE and factual consistency are nearly orthogonal—correction barely affects ROUGE ($\Delta < 0.002$), confirming that surface-level overlap metrics are inadequate for evaluating factual correction quality. We analyze 20 representative correction cases (10 baseline + 10 DCE) and discuss the structural reasons behind successful and failed corrections.

## 1. Introduction

Large language models (LLMs) have made significant progress in abstractive summarization, yet factual consistency remains a critical challenge. Generated summaries frequently contain entity substitutions, numerical errors, relational mismatches, and unsupported extrapolations [@cao2020factual, @liu2023improving]. Traditional evaluation metrics like ROUGE measure lexical overlap with reference summaries but fail to capture whether the generated text is faithful to the source document.

In the context of government report summarization (GovReport dataset), documents are exceptionally long, averaging thousands of tokens, making the factual consistency problem particularly acute. This work addresses three interconnected tasks: (1) building a baseline summarization and factual verification pipeline, (2) analyzing correction effectiveness across summary lengths and through case studies, and (3) designing an improved evidence retrieval method for better sentence-level factual detection.

Our contributions are threefold:

- We implement a complete “generate–detect–correct–evaluate” pipeline for long-document summarization, integrating chunk-based summarization, sentence-level NLI verification, and evidence-guided local correction.
- We propose **DCE** (Dual-Channel Evidence Retrieval), an original retrieval strategy featuring four novel mechanisms: dual-channel fusion (semantic + keyword), lightweight heuristic re-ranking, adaptive top-K selection, and conditional evidence expansion.
- We conduct systematic experiments on 500 GovReport samples comparing baseline retrieval against DCE, providing quantitative metrics, NLI label analysis, and 20 annotated correction cases.

## 2. Related Work

Factual consistency in summarization has attracted considerable attention. Cao et al. [@cao2020factual] pioneered the use of fact-checking models for post-editing abstractive summaries, framing error correction as a sequence-to-sequence task conditioned on source evidence. Liu et al. [@liu2023improving] proposed using natural language feedback to improve factual consistency, demonstrating that human-style critiques can guide models toward more faithful outputs. Wang et al. [@wang2024identifying] introduced a task taxonomy for grounding LLM-based inconsistency detection, categorizing errors into entity, relation, and discourse-level types.

For evidence retrieval in long-form summarization, dense retrieval models such as Sentence-BERT have been widely adopted for passage-level semantic search. Hybrid retrieval combining dense embeddings with sparse BM25 has shown improvements in open-domain QA, but its application to sentence-level factual verification remains underexplored. Beltagy et al. [@beltagy2021efficient] addressed the computational challenges of long-document processing through efficient attention mechanisms, forming the architectural foundation for many long-document summarizers.

Our DCE approach extends prior work by integrating multi-channel evidence retrieval with lightweight, zero-overhead re-ranking specifically designed for the sentence-level NLI setting, avoiding the computational cost of cross-encoder re-rankers while improving evidence quality.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1. Overview

Our end-to-end pipeline, orchestrated by `run_experiment.py`, processes GovReport samples through six stages: data loading → summarization → evidence retrieval → NLI verification → correction → re-evaluation. At a high level, the pipeline first generates a summary via chunk-based generation (§3.2), then retrieves evidence passages from the source document (§3.3), performs sentence-level NLI to detect unsupported claims (§3.4), applies evidence-guided local correction to flagged sentences (§3.5), and finally re-evaluates both factual consistency and ROUGE (§3.6). We use `Qwen/Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct` for both summarization and correction, `facebook/bart-large-mnli` for NLI, and `sentence-transformers/all-MiniLM-L6-v2` for embedding-based retrieval.

### 3.2. Chunk-Based Summarization

GovReport documents average several thousand tokens, far exceeding the context window of most LLMs. We adopt a chunk-and-fuse strategy:

1. `chunk_text()` splits the document by sentence boundaries into chunks of approximately 200 tokens.
2. Each chunk is independently summarized by Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct with `max_new_tokens=64`.
3. Local summaries are concatenated in order to produce the final summary.

The model supports fp32, fp16, and 8-bit precision modes, with safe loading and fallback managed by `utils/hf_helpers.py`.

### 3.3. Evidence Retrieval

We implement and compare two retrieval strategies, selectable via `--retrieval_strategy`.

#### 3.3.1. Baseline: Standard Retrieval

The baseline retriever `retrieval/retriever.py` uses:

- **Embedding**: Sentence-BERT (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) encoding of document passages.
- **Indexing**: FAISS with Flat, HNSW, or IVF index structures.
- **Optional BM25**: Sparse lexical retrieval for hybrid search.
- **Fixed top-K = 3**: Returns the three highest-scoring passages per query sentence.

Embedding results are cached via MD5 hashing to `data/emb_cache/` for reproducibility and speed.

#### 3.3.2. DCE: Dual-Channel Evidence Retrieval

Our proposed DCE strategy (`retrieval/advanced_retriever.py`) introduces four original mechanisms, summarized in Table 1:

**Table 1: DCE's four original mechanisms.**

| Mechanism              | Description                                                  |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Dual-Channel Fusion    | Semantic embeddings (SBERT + FAISS) and keyword BM25 independently retrieve candidates; scores are weighted (0.6 / 0.4) and merged with deduplication. |
| Lightweight Re-Ranking | Heuristic scoring via n-gram Jaccard overlap (60% weight), entity hit count, and positional coherence—zero additional model cost. |
| Adaptive Top-K         | Dynamic K based on sentence length: $k=3$ for short (<10 words), $k=5$ for medium, $k=7$ for long (>20 words). |
| Conditional Expansion  | When NLI confidence is low, the top-3 anchor passages are used to expand the candidate pool with their nearest neighbors. |

Each mechanism addresses a specific weakness of standard retrieval:

- **Dual-channel fusion** mitigates the vocabulary mismatch problem: dense embeddings capture semantic similarity while BM25 handles exact keyword and entity matching. The 0.6/0.4 weighting was determined through small-scale grid search on a held-out set of 20 samples.
- **Lightweight re-ranking** uses three zero-cost signals: (a) unigram and bigram Jaccard overlap between the query sentence and candidate passage (60% weight), (b) shared named entities and numerical expressions, and (c) positional coherence—passages closer to the source location of the sentence's topic are favored. This avoids the computational expense of cross-encoder re-rankers while providing meaningful quality improvement.
- **Adaptive Top-K** recognizes that sentence length correlates with information density: short sentences (<10 words) typically convey a single fact and need fewer evidence passages; long sentences (>20 words) often contain multiple clauses requiring broader evidence coverage.
- **Conditional expansion** activates only when the best NLI confidence score falls below 0.6, indicating evidence insufficiency. It retrieves the nearest-neighbor passages of the current top-3 in the FAISS index, expanding the candidate pool without incurring cost on well-supported sentences.

The core philosophy of DCE is **more precise evidence > more evidence**. By fusing complementary retrieval channels and applying heuristic re-ranking, DCE improves evidence relevance without incurring additional model inference cost. In our experiments, DCE adds approximately 15% overhead to the retrieval stage compared to baseline (0.32s vs. 0.28s per sentence on average), which is negligible relative to the NLI inference cost (0.85s per sentence).

### 3.4. Sentence-Level NLI Verification

Each summary sentence is paired with its retrieved evidence passages and evaluated by `facebook/bart-large-mnli`, which outputs a three-way classification:

- **ENTAILMENT**: evidence supports the sentence → `supported = True` (threshold ≥ 0.6)
- **CONTRADICTION**: evidence contradicts the sentence → `supported = False`
- **NEUTRAL**: insufficient evidence → `supported = False`

We use the `max` aggregation strategy: a sentence is considered supported if any evidence passage yields an ENTAILMENT score above the threshold. For efficiency, `check_with_evidence()` evaluates all evidence candidates for a sentence in a single batch.

### 3.5. Evidence-Guided Correction

Sentences flagged as unsupported undergo local correction via `Corrector.correct_batch()`:

1. A prompt is constructed combining the top-K evidence passages with the original sentence.
2. Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct (same model as summarization) generates a revised sentence.
3. Batch processing reduces LLM invocation overhead.
4. Supported sentences are preserved unchanged; only unsupported sentences are replaced.

After correction, the revised summary `corrected_pred` is re-evaluated through NLI to obtain `corrected_support_rate`.

### 3.6. Evaluation Metrics

We employ two complementary metric categories:

- **Factual Consistency**: Sentence-level support rate = (# supported sentences) / (# total sentences), computed both before and after correction.
- **Content Overlap**: ROUGE-1/2/L F1 scores against GovReport reference summaries, using `rouge-score` with stemming enabled.

These two categories are reported and analyzed separately, as they measure fundamentally different dimensions of summary quality.

Our initial bucketing scheme (1–3, 4–6, 7–10, 11–15, 16+) failed to discriminate among GovReport summaries because all 500 samples fall into the 16+ bucket (min 83, max 1437, median 352.0 sentences). Longer summaries derive larger correction gains under both strategies, and DCE consistently delivers higher gains than baseline across all length buckets.**

Four clear patterns emerge:
1. **Support rate declines with length:** Longer summaries have lower pre-correction support rates under both strategies. The shortest quartile (83–240 sentences) achieves ~0.71 support, while the longest quartile (509–1437) drops to ~0.63–0.65. This ~6pp decline suggests that longer summaries contain proportionally more sentences with insufficient or ambiguous evidence coverage.
2. **Correction gains increase with length:** Under baseline, correction gain grows from +0.0757 (shortest) to +0.0869 (longest); under DCE, from +0.0794 to +0.0938. DCE consistently achieves higher gains (+0.5–0.7pp more than baseline per bucket). The longest summaries benefit most from correction because they have the most room for improvement.
3. **ROUGE declines sharply with length:** ROUGE-1 F1 drops from 0.2445 (shortest quartile) to 0.0802 (longest quartile)—a 67% relative decline. This is a direct consequence of the ROUGE recall denominator growing with summary length: when both reference and hypothesis contain hundreds of sentences, unmatched n-grams dominate the denominator, systematically compressing F1. This strong length-dependence confirms that **ROUGE should not be interpreted as an absolute quality measure for long summaries**.
4. **NLI label distribution is length-stable:** Across percentile buckets, ENTAILMENT/NEUTRAL/CONTRADICTION ratios remain within ±2pp of the global average, indicating that the *type* of error does not change appreciably with summary length—only the *rate* of unsupported content increases.

### 6.3. Analysis

Several structural patterns emerge from the 20 cases (Tables 6 and 7):

- **Improvement is the norm:** >98% of samples across both strategies show support rate gains. Baseline success cases average +0.3273 improvement; DCE success cases average +0.3229.
- **Ceiling effect:** Samples with pre-correction support >0.75 gain minimally from correction, as few sentences require modification.
- **Floor effect:** Samples with pre-correction support <0.40 gain the most (+0.29 to +0.37), indicating that correction is most impactful when the original summary contains substantial unsupported content.
- **DCE exhibits stronger polarization:** DCE failure cases show an average drop of -0.0785 vs. baseline's -0.0185, suggesting that higher-quality evidence creates a sharper decision boundary: successful corrections benefit more, and failed corrections suffer more.
- **ROUGE and support rate are nearly orthogonal:** Across all 20 cases, ROUGE-1 $\Delta$ ranges from -0.0550 to +0.0146, mostly within ±0.003. The most notable counterexample is DCE Case 119, where ROUGE-1 improves by +0.0146 but support drops by -0.1223—a clear illustration that “writing more like the reference” does not imply “writing more faithfully to the source.”
- **Concurrent improvement is possible but rare:** Only ~5–10% of cases achieve simultaneous gains in both support rate and ROUGE (e.g., Baseline Case 305, DCE Case 305). These cases typically have moderate pre-correction support (0.40–0.50) and require only minor terminological adjustments.
- **DCE failures are more instructive:** DCE failure cases (avg. -0.0785) exhibit a wider range than baseline failures (avg. -0.0185). Case 119 (-0.1223 support, +0.0146 ROUGE) is particularly informative: the DCE corrector, guided by precise but narrow evidence, over-polished the summary to resemble the reference style at the expense of factual grounding.

### 6.4. Illustrative Semantic Example

To make the ROUGE–support orthogonality concrete, consider the following qualitative example from DCE Case 305. The original summary contained the sentence:

> “The report examines law enforcement challenges and training programs for officers.”

The NLI module flagged this as NEUTRAL because the evidence passages described *specific* training types (de-escalation, crisis intervention) and challenges (staffing shortages, mental health encounters), while the sentence used generic terms. The corrector rewrote it to:

> “The report examines training programs such as de-escalation and crisis intervention for law enforcement officers facing mental health encounters.”

The revised sentence gained ENTAILMENT (support rate +0.3052), but its ROUGE-1 overlap with the reference hardly changed because the reference used yet another paraphrase. This micro-level example illustrates the fundamental mechanism underlying our results: **factual correction replaces generic claims with evidence-anchored specifics, which ROUGE—being a surface-form overlap metric—cannot distinguish from the original generic phrasing**.

## 7. Discussion

### 7.1. Why Correction is Effective

The pipeline achieves consistent gains (+10.03pp baseline, +10.96pp DCE) across >98% of samples because:
- **Evidence-constrained generation:** The corrector rewrites sentences conditioned on retrieved evidence, not free-form generation.
- **Local revision:** Only unsupported sentences are modified, preserving the overall summary structure and avoiding semantic drift.
- **Sentence-level granularity:** Fine-grained detection ensures precise targeting of problematic content.

### 7.2. DCE's Unique Value Proposition

DCE demonstrates value in three dimensions:
1. **Stricter judgments** (ENTAILMENT reduced by 6.16pp): Fewer false positives mean the “needs correction” signal is more reliable.
2. **Larger correction gain** ($\Delta = +0.1096$ vs. $+0.1003$): Higher-quality evidence provides better guidance for the corrector.
3. **Sharper decision boundary:** Stronger polarization between success and failure cases indicates that the $\text{evidence quality} \rightarrow \text{NLI confidence} \rightarrow \text{correction outcome}$ chain is more stable under DCE.

### 7.3. Why ROUGE is Nearly Invariant and Why It is Low

ROUGE changes by less than 0.002 because: (1) only 23–35% of sentences are modified; (2) rewritten sentences maintain similar length and structure; (3) ROUGE measures lexical overlap with the reference summary, which local factual corrections do not significantly alter. **This reinforces that ROUGE is not a suitable metric for evaluating factual correction quality.**

The low absolute ROUGE-1 F1 (0.1495)—far below the 0.3–0.5 range typical of news summarization benchmarks—has five technical explanations:
1. **Extreme summary length:** GovReport summaries average ~397 sentences. ROUGE recall is computed over all n-grams in the reference, and when both reference and hypothesis are very long, the denominator is enormous, systematically depressing F1.
2. **Single-reference evaluation:** We compare against only one reference summary per document. Long summaries admit many valid phrasings; a single reference cannot cover all acceptable variants, underestimating recall. Multi-reference ROUGE typically yields 10–20pp higher scores.
3. **Chunk-based generation bias:** Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct with `max_new_tokens=64` produces extractive, per-chunk compressions rather than holistic abstractions. This “semi-extractive” style diverges lexically from human-written reference summaries.
4. **Same-model correction:** Using the same model for both generation and correction perpetuates generation biases; an independent corrector might produce text with different lexical characteristics.
5. **ROUGE is inherently unsuitable for factual evaluation:** As demonstrated by our results and argued by prior work [@cao2020factual, @liu2023improving], ROUGE measures word overlap, not factual correctness. The near-zero correlation between ROUGE-$\Delta$ and support-rate-$\Delta$ empirically confirms this distinction.

Consequently, we treat ROUGE as a **style/convergence sanity check** rather than a quality metric. Factual consistency is evaluated solely through sentence-level support rate; ROUGE is reported only to confirm that correction does not catastrophically degrade surface text quality.

### 7.4. Limitations

- High NEUTRAL rate (63–69%): Many sentences lack sufficient evidence, suggesting retrieval coverage can be improved through query rewriting or iterative expansion.
- Single-pass correction: No iterative verification loop; future work could adopt multi-round self-verification.
- Shared summarization and correction model: May propagate generation biases; using an independent correction model could improve outcomes.
- No human evaluation: Automatic NLI may misclassify sentences; human annotation would validate the NLI judgments.
- Length-based analysis can be extended: While our percentile-based bucketing revealed clear length-dependent trends, further segmentation by token count or abstractiveness could yield additional insights.

## 8. Conclusion

We presented an end-to-end pipeline for factual consistency evaluation and correction in long-document summarization, introducing DCE—a novel dual-channel evidence retrieval strategy with four original mechanisms. Experiments on 500 GovReport samples demonstrate: (1) sentence-level NLI effectively detects factual risks (baseline 67.23%, DCE 64.73% pre-correction support); (2) automatic correction significantly improves factual consistency (+10.03pp baseline, +10.96pp DCE), benefiting >98% of samples; (3) DCE achieves greater correction gain through stricter and more precise evidence retrieval; and (4) ROUGE and factual consistency are nearly orthogonal metrics, confirming that surface-level overlap alone cannot evaluate correction quality. Twenty annotated case studies further reveal the structural dynamics of successful versus failed corrections, providing a foundation for future work on iterative verification and retrieval-augmented correction.
